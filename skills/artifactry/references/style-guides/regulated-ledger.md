# Regulated Ledger

Regulated Ledger is a trust-first executive style for artifacts that must feel precise, controlled, and decision-ready. Use it for investor updates, board decks, financial summaries, operating memos, market explainers, risk reviews, and internal policy documents. It inherits from institutional finance, enterprise systems, regulated product UI, and large-organization documentation patterns in the local design corpus.

## Core Idea

Everything should feel reviewed before it is shown. The canvas is mostly white or very pale gray. The accent color is a scarce blue signal used only for the most important number, active route, selected row, or page section marker. The layout must make the reader feel that claims are auditable and that numbers can be trusted.

## Visual Signature

- White canvas with soft gray bands and restrained blue highlights.
- Large calm sans headlines, not heavy display shouting.
- Mono numerals for dates, percentages, ranks, page numbers, and metrics.
- Hairline dividers, thin table rules, small labels, and controlled card radius.
- Evidence cards that look like financial product surfaces, not decorative tiles.
- Occasional dark inverse page for a major risk, conclusion, or executive summary.

## Palette

Use pure white, off-white, cool gray, dark ink, and one blue action signal. Avoid adding green/red except for semantic up/down or risk states. Yellow may appear only as a tiny alert marker. Dark pages should be near-black with blue or white evidence lines, never neon.

Suggested roles:

- Canvas: white or `#f7f8fa`
- Ink: near-black
- Body: blue-gray
- Rule: cool gray hairline
- Primary: institutional blue
- Semantic: green/red only inside values

## Typography

Use one strong sans family with a mono companion. Headlines run large, calm, and regular weight. Body text should be sturdy but not loud. Kicker labels are uppercase or small caps only when they behave like report metadata. Do not use decorative serif display.

## Layout System

Use a 12-column grid with generous outer margins. Keep content blocks aligned to a visible report rhythm. Prefer rows, tables, metric cards, comparison columns, and indexed sections. White space should communicate confidence, not emptiness. Every page needs a stable header/footer system: document title or section on the left, page number or date on the right, source/footer at bottom.

## Slide Recipes

- Cover: title left, one key metric or card stack right, sparse metadata footer.
- Executive summary: 3 to 5 numbered claims in bordered rows.
- Big number: one mono value, one sentence, one source line.
- Risk register: severity pill, risk title, owner, mitigation, status.
- Comparison: two equal cards with identical structure; blue marks the recommended path.
- Timeline: dated rows with mono dates and hairline separators.
- Dashboard: limited to 4 to 6 metrics, never a crowded BI screenshot.
- Closer: one decision statement plus next-step checklist.

## DOCX Translation

For DOCX, translate style into margins, headings, tables, callouts, and page headers. Use blue heading accents sparingly. Tables should be the strongest component: shaded header row, thin gray borders, mono values. Do not attempt gradient or heavy visual effects in Word.

## Agent Rules

- Make every claim traceable to a source, note, or input line.
- If the Markdown has numbers, promote the most important one into a metric surface.
- If the Markdown is vague, ask for audience and decision goal before rendering.
- Do not use random icons. Use text labels, dots, pills, and status markers.
- Reject slides that look like generic SaaS cards without evidence hierarchy.

## Avoid

Avoid playful colors, collage, thick shadows, serif magazine drama, busy gradients, sticker-like icons, and overdecorated charts. This style succeeds when it feels like a serious report became visually clear.
